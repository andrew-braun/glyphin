# Latin Diacritics Reading Curriculum Notes

This course is a representative multilingual v1 for English-literate learners
who already know the basic Latin alphabet but need to decode common marks and
special letters in European environmental print.

## Course Boundary

- Language/script: multilingual European Latin-script diacritics, `mul-Latn`,
  ISO 15924 `Latn`.
- Target learner: traveler or culture learner reading menus, hotel labels,
  transit signs, street names, and simple food labels.
- Target domains: food and cafe menus, transit signage, hotel/facility labels,
  street names, and everyday labels.
- In scope for v1: acute, grave, circumflex, umlaut/diaeresis, cedilla, tilde,
  base Latin carriers, and late eszett recognition.
- Out of scope for v1: complete orthography courses for any single language,
  handwriting, production spelling, advanced morphology, and less common marks
  such as caron, ogonek, ring, breve, and double acute.

## Sequencing Rationale

- Start with `café` because it is visible across languages and already familiar
  to many English speakers.
- Pair `entrada` and `salida` early because sign pairs are excellent first
  decoding wins even without diacritics.
- Teach marks by visual family, then bind them to language-specific functions
  cautiously.
- Keep language context visible on anchors so learners do not assume one mark has
  the same pronunciation or grammatical role everywhere.

## Validation Notes

- Segmentation review: verify NFC storage and decomposition metadata for all
  precomposed characters used in anchors.
- Pronunciation review: assign reviewers for at least Spanish, French, German,
  and Portuguese before learner-facing pronunciation copy ships.
- Cultural review: confirm that anchors are natural in visible traveler contexts
  and not chosen only because they are convenient examples.
- License review: keep Wiktionary, OpenStreetMap, and corpus-derived content out
  of shipped examples until attribution and derived-data obligations are clear.
