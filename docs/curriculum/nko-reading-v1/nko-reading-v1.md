# N'Ko Reading Curriculum Notes

This durable course note records the v1 boundary and bootstrap decisions for an
N'Ko reading course.

## Course Boundary

- Language/script: N'Ko language context in N'Ko script, `nqo-Nkoo`, ISO 15924
  `Nkoo`.
- Target learner: community learner, heritage learner, West African script
  enthusiast, or reader who wants to recognize modern N'Ko text.
- Target domains: community literacy materials, dictionary headwords, digital
  text, cultural identity text, and short public labels.
- In scope for v1: RTL reading, core letters, selected vowel letters, early
  combining-mark awareness, script-name anchor, and app-authored examples.
- Out of scope for v1: full Manding grammar, full tone pedagogy, production
  spelling, broad corpus mining, and copied external examples.

## Sequencing Rationale

- Frequency sources: Unicode and CLDR metadata for inventory, font references for
  mark rendering, Wikimedia text for discovery only, and app-authored examples
  for shipped content.
- First-session decoding target: `ߒߞߏ` because it is compact, memorable, and
  forces correct RTL rendering.
- Stage 1 goal: establish directionality, script identity, and a few core
  letters.
- Stage 2 goal: introduce combining marks and identity text cautiously.
- Stage 3 goal: add reviewer-selected domain text after font and mark QA.

## Script Notes

- N'Ko is RTL and uses combining marks that can be small at lesson-card sizes.
- Tone and nasal marks are not decorative; they need explicit display and
  pedagogy decisions.
- Language-variety scope should be approved before the course claims broader
  Manding-language coverage.

## Validation Notes

- Segmentation review: verify grapheme clusters with combining marks and RTL
  punctuation.
- Pronunciation review: confirm tone-mark explanations, transliteration, and
  language-variety assumptions.
- Cultural review: ensure script-identity and public-label examples are
  respectful and locally appropriate.
- License review: keep external digital text discovery-only until share-alike and
  attribution implications are resolved.
