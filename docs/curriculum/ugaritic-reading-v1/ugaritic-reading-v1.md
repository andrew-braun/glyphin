# Ugaritic Reading Curriculum Notes

This durable course note records the v1 boundary and bootstrap decisions for a
Ugaritic reading course.

## Course Boundary

- Language/script: Ugaritic in Ugaritic alphabetic cuneiform, `uga-Ugar`, ISO
  15924 `Ugar`.
- Target learner: ancient Near East learner, museum visitor, or Semitic-language
  beginner who wants to recognize Ugaritic signs and short tablet words.
- Target domains: alphabetic tablets, mythological texts, royal and city names,
  museum labels, and transliteration workbenches.
- In scope for v1: core alphabetic signs, word divider, short consonant-word
  anchors, aleph and ayin awareness, and culturally reviewed divine-name terms.
- Out of scope for v1: complete myths, vocalization training, grammar, damaged
  tablet restoration, and exhaustive alphabet teaching before anchors.

## Sequencing Rationale

- Frequency sources: Unicode metadata, Ugaritic corpus references, discovery-only
  lexicon checks, font checks, and app-authored examples for shipped content.
- First-session decoding target: `𐎟`, `𐎎𐎍𐎋`, and `𐎊𐎎`, because they are
  compact and make word division plus consonantal reading visible.
- Stage 1 goal: establish left-to-right tablet word segmentation and common
  consonants.
- Stage 2 goal: add sacred and cultural terms with respectful framing.
- Stage 3 goal: broaden the sign set only after ambiguity and gloss caveats are
  clear.

## Script Notes

- Ugaritic is alphabetic cuneiform, so it looks like cuneiform but behaves more
  like a consonantal alphabet for beginner segmentation.
- Vowels are not generally written in ordinary consonant sequences, and aleph
  signs require specialist policy.
- The corpus is small and genre-specific. Frequency is not equivalent to modern
  living-language utility.
- Divine and mythological names need cultural framing and expert review.

## Validation Notes

- Segmentation review: verify word dividers, sign order, transliteration, and
  aleph or ayin distinctions.
- Pronunciation review: label any pronunciation as reconstructed or pedagogical.
- Cultural review: handle divine names and mythological terms respectfully.
- License review: keep corpus and lexicon derived strings out of shipped content
  until reuse obligations are approved.
