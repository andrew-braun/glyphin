# IPA Reading Curriculum Notes

IPA Reading is a notation course, not a language course. It teaches learners to
decode common IPA symbols found in dictionaries and language-learning materials.

## Course Boundary

- Language/script: undetermined language with Latin-extension notation,
  `und-Latn`, ISO 15924 `Latn`.
- Target learner: English-literate language learner who wants to read
  pronunciation transcriptions.
- Target domains: dictionary pronunciation, textbooks, pronunciation guides,
  learner notes, and cross-language examples.
- In scope for v1: common English-dictionary IPA consonants and vowels, brackets,
  stress marks, schwa, and a few contrast families.
- Out of scope for v1: full articulatory phonetics, tone systems, rare symbols,
  clicks, ejectives, narrow transcription, and required audio production.

## Sequencing Rationale

- Start with familiar short English words so the learner can separate spelling
  from phonetic notation.
- Teach positive-transfer symbols first, then high-payoff unfamiliar vowels and
  fricatives.
- Introduce stress and schwa early because they are common in dictionary entries.
- Defer theoretical labels unless they help distinguish two visible symbols.

## Validation Notes

- Segmentation review: decide when multi-symbol sequences such as diphthongs and
  affricates should be taught as chunks rather than separate symbols.
- Pronunciation review: use a phonetics reviewer or speech-language specialist to
  validate anchor transcriptions and English accent assumptions.
- Cultural review: not required for ordinary English anchor words, but avoid
  implying one English accent is universal.
- License review: do not copy dictionary entries, IPA chart layout, or reference
  prose into shipped lessons. Use app-authored examples.
